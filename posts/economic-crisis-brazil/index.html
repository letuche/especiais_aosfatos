Brazil, out of the curve

Where the country stands in the global economy?

Image: Fabio Rodrigues Pozzebom/ABr

By Sérgio Spagnuolo
Design and graphics by Volt Data Lab
May/2016

Brazil's interim president, Michel Temer, has said his government will reign over the national deficit, the debt, the unemployment and the rising inflation. The goal, according to his economic team, is to resume growth and attract investments.

In the meantime, engulfed in a severe political crisis, the country has seen in the past couple of years a sharp rise in consumer prices, millions of people losing their jobs, a growing national debt and a shrinking Gross Domestic Product (GDP). But, if things are bad in Brazil now, how does the rest of the world fare against South America’s biggest economy?

Aos Fatos, in partnership with Volt Data Lab, prepared a series of graphics to show where the country stands in the global scene, according to some of the most important economic indicators: GDP growth, debt over GDP ratio, unemployment and inflation rate.

The Brazilian GDP trajectory is far from the world median, as the country is currently in a deep recession. According to the International Monetary Fund, Brazil would only have some better perspective from 2019 - but growth would still float around meager 2%.

With the GDP slowdown, unemployment in Brazil grew in 2015 from the previous year's levels, when the country registered 5% unemployment - considered low by the International Labour Organization (ILO).

The percentage of the people without jobs is now worse than it was in 2012, aroung 11% in the first quarter, totaling 10 million people. A high level indeed, but not an anomaly, including among some developed countries.

The data are not adjusted to be directly comparable among the countries, but rather to outline the performance within every country. To see comparable data, check the ILO website.

Inflation is soaring in Brazil - way ahead the world's 3% median - making products and services more expensive, and affecting credit and investiments.

As comparison, the Central Bank ceiling target is 6,5%.

The good news is that the IMF forecasts a gradual slowdown in the next few years.

Although debt is needed to fund the government and cover the public deficit, when in excess it can burden a country's finance.

Brazil's net debt over its GDP is not as massive as those of several developed countries, like the US or others from the European Union, but it is some 20 points ahead of world's median.

References

The data shown in the graphics might be different from official government numbers, because of separate methodology from the IMF or from Trending Economics, the main sources in this report.

The reference point is the "median", value that separates the upper half from the bottom half.

The main graphics were made with the javascript library D3.js, while the line charts were built using Morris.js.
